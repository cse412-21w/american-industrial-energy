Carbon dioxide emissions, concentrations, and temperature

Since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution in the mid-18th century, the consumption of fossil fuels for the production of energy has steadily and nearly exponentially risen, and along with it the emissions of carbon dioxide.
Future emissions will depend on international treaties, national policies, and individual behavior.

Anthropogenic emissions of carbon dioxide have led to a steady rise in the atmospheric concentration of CO2 from about 278 ppm before the Industrial Revolution to about 414 ppm in 2020. Future concentrations depend on the emissions pathway chosen by humankind.

Carbon dioxide is the primary greenhouse gas. Its increasing concentration has led to an increase in temperatures across the globe. Future
temperatures will depend on the emissions pathway. Shown are global average temperatures in degrees Celsius relative to their average between 1850 and 1900.
The goal of the Paris Agreement (2015) is to limit global warming to well below 2, preferably to 1.5 degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels.
Only the low emission path will fully meet this goal.
Source: IPCC, NOAA/NCEI

Power plants in the U.S.

The map shows all power plants in the United States, colored by their energy source, with the size of the circles corresponding to the energy produced.
Coal-fired power plants are especially common in the traditional Rust Belt.
Wind farms are most common in all states just east of the Rocky Mountains.
Solar power is mostly produced in California, Arizona, and some other southern states.

Energy sources and users

This chart shows the different types of energy sources on the left and the end users by sector on the right.
Petroleum (gasoline, diesel fuel, and kerosene) is used mostly in the transportation sector, but also directly in industry.
Natural gas is used directly in the industrial, residential, and commercial sectors as well as for generation of electricity.
All other sources are primarily used for generation of electricity.

Changes in electricity generation over the recent decade

From 2008 to 2019, the use of coal for the generation of electricity was cut in half, whereas the use of natural gas doubled.
Solar and wind power generation has increased dramatically, but all renewable energy sources (hydroelectric, wind, solar, and geothermal) still produce less electricity than coal alone.
